  3. Two numbers \(a\) and \(b\) are said to be relatively prime if \(\gcd(a,b)=1\).
    1. Demonstrate that \(77\) and \(45\) either are or are not relatively prime by factoring them to find their GCD.
    2. Use the \(\gcd\) algorithm to determine if \(77\) and \(45\) are relatively prime.
    3. The number \(23\) is prime, so Jed says that \(23\) must be relatively prime with every other positive integer. Give a counterexample to Jed's conjecture.
    4. Despite overgeneralizing, Jed was on to something. Improve his conjecture about primes and relative primes.

  11. Read: Henri Poincaré, Science and Reason, Book I, Chapter IV: “Chance”, Sections I and II.
    1. Briefly descrbe the three examples of fortuitous chance given by Poincaré.
    2. Poincaré writes:
      But, even if it were the case that the natural laws had no longer any secret for us, we could still only know the initial situation approximately. [...] it may happen that small differences in the initial conditions produce very great ones in the final phenomena. A small error in the former will produce an enormous error in the latter. Prediction becomes impossible, and we have the fortuitous phenomenon.
      This describes what has come to be known as sensitive dependence on initial conditions, or the butterfly effect. We will be using data to make predictions in this course. How might sensitive dependence on initial conditions affect our predictions?
